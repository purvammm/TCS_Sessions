A quick refresher before we critique anything

Traditional SDLC — the six phases

Every software project — whether you call it Waterfall, Agile, or anything else — moves through the same six broad phases. The difference between methodologies is the order, the size of each step, and how often you loop back.

📋

1 · Requirements

Talk to users, write down what the system should do. "What are we building?"

🧭

2 · Design

Decide the architecture, the data model, the modules. "How will it be structured?"

⌨️

3 · Implementation

Write the code. "Build it."

🧪

4 · Testing

Verify it works — unit tests, integration tests, QA. "Does it behave correctly?"

🚀

5 · Deployment

Release it to production where users can reach it. "Ship it."

🔧

6 · Maintenance

Fix bugs, add small features, keep the lights on. "Keep it running."

The whole story in one line Agile doesn't skip any of these phases. It just runs all six in a tight loop — a 1-to-4 week loop called a Sprint — instead of one long single-pass line.

The "do it all in order" model

Waterfall Model

Waterfall is the classical sequential model. You finish each phase completely before starting the next — like water cascading down a series of pools, never flowing back up. It was popularised in the 1970s by Winston Royce, borrowing from manufacturing-era project management.

How a Waterfall project actually feels You spend the first 2–3 months gathering and signing off requirements. Then 2–3 months on design documents. Then 4–6 months coding. Then 1–2 months in QA. Then you ship — and the customer sees a running system for the first time nearly a year after the project started. If their needs shifted in the meantime — and they almost always do — that's a problem the model doesn't gracefully handle.
When Waterfall genuinely works When requirements are truly known and stable up-front — building a bridge, designing a chip, certifying a medical device for the FDA. Software almost never has that luxury, which is exactly why we needed something else.

Why Waterfall breaks down for modern software

Disadvantages of Waterfall

Three structural problems make Waterfall a bad fit for software where you're learning as you go:

⏰

Late feedback

The customer doesn't see anything working until weeks or months in. By then, the original assumptions may already be obsolete. The first sprint of Agile delivers working software; Waterfall's first year may not.

🔁

Hard to change

A requirement change late in the cycle ripples back through every prior phase — and there's no efficient way to absorb it. Change requests become expensive battles, often refused, often the reason projects fail.

💣

Big-bang testing

Testing only begins after months of coding. Bugs found at that stage are 10–100× more expensive to fix than the same bug caught during design. The longer a defect lives, the more code depends on it.

The remedy in one sentence "Plan the plan, build the thing, hope it's right" is too risky for software. The fix: make the loop small, learn quickly, and adjust. That's the Agile thesis.
Honest disclaimer Waterfall isn't dead. Regulated, fixed-scope projects (medical devices, aerospace flight controls, certain government contracts) still use it — sometimes because compliance demands it. But for the kind of business software TCS associates write day-to-day, Agile is the default for good reason.

The mindset that changed software

What is Agile?

Agile is not a tool, not a single methodology, and not a piece of software. It's a set of values for how to build software when the requirements are uncertain — and in software, they almost always are. It was formalised in February 2001 by seventeen practitioners who met at a ski lodge in Snowbird, Utah and wrote what is now called the Agile Manifesto.

🔁

Iterative

Work in short, repeating cycles — usually 1 to 4 weeks. Each cycle ends with feedback that shapes the next one.

📦

Incremental

Each cycle produces a working, shippable slice of the product — not a document, not a half-built piece, but something a real user could touch.

🧭

Adaptive

Change direction based on what you learned in the previous cycle. The plan is allowed — encouraged — to evolve.

The core assumption Agile assumes you'll learn things during the project that change your plan. The shorter the loop, the faster you learn, and the less waste from being wrong. That's the whole argument.

A worked example of "left over right" Imagine a build pipeline is broken on a Tuesday afternoon. The process says raise a ticket and wait for a DevOps engineer. The Agile instinct says walk over (or DM) the closest person who can help, fix it together, and update the process afterwards. The ticket isn't wrong — but the interaction unblocks faster.

The values made operational

The 12 Agile Principles — the heartbeat six

The Manifesto expands its 4 values into 12 principles that describe what teams should actually do. Six of them carry most of the weight day-to-day:

1
Highest priority: satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery.

Ship something useful in the first sprint. Then keep shipping. Don't disappear for six months and reappear with a final product.

2
Welcome changing requirements, even late in development.

Treat a late change as a competitive advantage — it means the product gets better — instead of as a contract violation.

3
Deliver working software frequently — every few weeks, ideally shorter.

The shorter the gap between releases, the faster you learn what's working and what isn't.

4
Business people and developers must work together daily.

The "throw a spec over the wall" model is over. The PO is part of the team, in the same chat, in the same standup.

5
Working software is the primary measure of progress.

Not lines of code, not pages of design, not story points done. Working software, in the hands of users.

6
At regular intervals, the team reflects and tunes its behaviour.

This is the principle that the Retrospective (slide 15) exists to live out.

The other six in one line Build projects around motivated individuals · face-to-face conversation is best · sustainable pace · technical excellence and good design · simplicity (the art of maximising the work not done) · the best architectures emerge from self-organising teams. (Full text at agilemanifesto.org.)

Same six SDLC phases, very different paths

Agile vs Waterfall

Both models walk through requirements, design, build, test, deploy, maintain. The difference is how often the loop runs and where in that loop the customer lives.

DimensionWaterfallAgile (Scrum)
Cycle lengthMonths to a year+ (one pass)1–4 weeks per sprint, then repeat

Change requestsExpensive, often refusedWelcomed between sprints

Best fitFixed scope, regulated, predictableUncertain, evolving, product-focused
Don't get tripped up Agile is not "no planning." It's "planning in small, frequent chunks." Every sprint has a plan — it just covers two weeks instead of two years.

Agile is a family, not a single recipe

Agile flavors

"Agile" is a mindset. Several concrete frameworks implement it — some emphasize process, some emphasize engineering practices, some emphasize flow, some emphasize scaling. The most-used ones at a glance:

Process

Scrum

Sprint-based delivery with defined roles, artifacts and ceremonies. The most widely adopted Agile framework — used by an estimated 80%+ of Agile teams. Today's deep dive.

Engineering

XP

Extreme Programming. A programmer-centric flavor focused on engineering practices: pair programming, TDD, continuous integration, refactoring, simple design.

Flow

Kanban

No fixed sprints. Visualise work as cards on a board, set a work-in-progress (WIP) limit, and pull the next card only when capacity frees up. Great for support & ops teams.

Scaling

SAFe

Scaled Agile Framework. For 50–500+ engineers across many teams. Adds program-level coordination layers (Agile Release Trains, PI Planning) on top of Scrum.

Today's session zooms in on Scrum because that's what most enterprise teams (TCS included) use. The engineering practices from XP — especially TDD — layer on top of Scrum naturally; the next slide gives both a closer look.
Kanban & SAFe — awareness, not depth Recognise the names if they come up in interviews. Kanban = continuous flow with WIP limits. SAFe = Scrum scaled across many teams. Neither is on the practical exam for this session.

Two engineering practices every Agile developer should know

XP & TDD up close

XP — Extreme Programming

Programmer-centric: how the engineering work itself is done. "Extreme" means: take the things software dev should already do, and crank them to 11.

  • Pair programming — two devs, one keyboard. Continuous review.
  • Test-Driven Development — see the right column.
  • Continuous Integration — every commit goes through the build & tests.
  • Refactoring — improve code structure constantly, without changing behaviour.
  • Simple design — do the simplest thing that could work, no speculative complexity.
  • Small releases — release early, release often.

TDD — Test-Driven Development

Write the test first, then write just enough code to make it pass. A three-step cycle:

🔴

Red

Write a failing test. The feature doesn't exist yet, so the test fails.

🟢

Green

Write the minimum code to make the test pass. Ugly is fine.

🔵

Refactor

Clean up the code while keeping the test green. Repeat.

Why TDD matters in your work TDD isn't exclusive to XP — Scrum teams use it constantly, and so should you in your assignments. The discipline of "test first" prevents the classic "I'll write the tests later" (i.e. never) anti-pattern, and the code that emerges from TDD is naturally testable because it had to be.

The Scrum balance in one sentence Inside a sprint, the goal doesn't change. Between sprints, anything can change. That's how Scrum gives the team stability to focus, and the product space to evolve.

Three roles · three responsibilities · no overlap

Scrum Roles

Owns the WHAT & WHY

Product Owner (PO)

Maintains the Product Backlog and prioritises it. Decides what goes into a sprint. Faces the customer and business. One person, never a committee. The PO is empowered to say "yes, build this" or "no, not yet."

Owns the HOW

Scrum Master (SM)

Coaches the team on Scrum, facilitates ceremonies, removes blockers. A servant-leader — not a project manager, not a boss. Protects the team from outside interruption during a sprint.

Owns the DOING

Development Team

Cross-functional (devs, testers, designers — whatever the work needs), self-organising, typically 3–9 people. The team decides how to split tasks. No team lead within the Dev Team — they're all peers on the work.

A few rules of thumb Every Scrum team has all three roles — even a four-person startup. One person can sometimes hold two roles, but PO + Dev Team is forbidden (conflict of interest — the PO would unconsciously protect their own team's workload), and PO + Scrum Master is discouraged (the PO advocates for the product, the SM advocates for the team — different incentives).

The three "things" Scrum produces and uses

Scrum Artifacts

📚

Product Backlog

The master list of everything that might ever go into the product. Ordered top-to-bottom by priority. Owned and groomed by the PO. Each item is a User Story:

"As a returning customer, I want to save my shipping address, so that checkout is faster next time."

📋

Sprint Backlog

The subset of stories the team commits to deliver in this sprint, pulled from the top of the Product Backlog during Sprint Planning. Doesn't change mid-sprint. Owned by the Dev Team.

📦

Increment

The actual working software produced by the end of the sprint. It must be Done — coded, tested, integrated, potentially shippable. Every sprint produces an Increment, even if the team chooses not to release externally each time.

A useful analogy Product Backlog is the kitchen menu (everything the restaurant could cook). Sprint Backlog is tonight's order list (what's actively being cooked right now). Increment is the plated meal that goes out to the table.

Four meetings · each with one clear purpose

Scrum Ceremonies

Scrum has exactly four meetings. Every one has a fixed time-box and a fixed goal — if a meeting doesn't fit one of these, it shouldn't exist on a Scrum team's calendar.

Start of sprint

📅 Sprint Planning

What will we deliver and how? The PO walks through top-priority backlog items; the team estimates and commits. Time-box: ~2 hours per week of sprint length.

Every weekday

🌅 Daily Scrum

15 minutes, same time daily, often standing up. Each dev answers three questions: (1) What did I do yesterday? (2) What will I do today? (3) Any blockers? A team sync — not a status report to a manager.

End of sprint

🎬 Sprint Review

Demo the working Increment to stakeholders. They give feedback that flows back into the Product Backlog. Time-box: ~1 hour per week of sprint length.

Right after Review

🪞 Sprint Retrospective

Inward-looking. What went well? What didn't? What will we change next sprint? Team only, no outsiders. Time-box: ~45 min per week of sprint length.

The most common confusion in this slide Sprint Review = look outward at the product, with stakeholders. Sprint Retrospective = look inward at the process, with the team only. People mix these up — they're very different meetings with very different audiences and very different outputs.

How Scrum teams estimate work

Story Points & Planning Poker

"How big is this story?" Not in hours — in Story Points. Story points are a relative size: a 3-point story is roughly three times the effort of a 1-point story. Why relative? Because humans are bad at absolute time estimates but reasonable at comparing two things side-by-side.

Planning Poker — the estimation game

1
PO reads the story aloud — team asks clarifying questions.
2
Each developer privately picks a card with their point estimate.
3
Everyone reveals at the same time — no anchoring on the loudest voice.
4
If estimates differ wildly (one says 2, another says 13), the lowest and highest share their reasoning. New context surfaces.
5
Re-vote until reasonably converged. The number matters less than the conversation that produced it.
Why this works Planning Poker forces every developer to think about every story, prevents the senior-est person from dominating, and surfaces unknown unknowns. A wide spread of votes is a feature — it means the team doesn't yet share the same picture, and you need to talk about that before committing.

The reason story points beat "give me the hours"

Why relative estimation > time estimation

⏱️ Time estimates (hours / days)

  • Different devs work at different speeds — same task, different hours from different people.
  • Anchor to the past ("last time it took 4 hours") — but the last time isn't this time.
  • Implicitly commits to a deadline — and management treats the estimate as a promise.
  • Psychologically harder than comparing two stories side-by-side.
  • Inflates under social pressure ("I'll say 6 hours to be safe").

📐 Story points (relative)

  • Team-relative scale — same people estimate, same team interprets.
  • Captures complexity + risk + effort, not just clock time.
  • Over 3–4 sprints, the team's velocity (avg. points completed per sprint) becomes a reliable forecasting tool.
  • Decouples size from delivery date — the date is a function of size ÷ velocity.
  • Easier for new joiners to plug into ("this feels like that 5-point one we did last sprint").
The honest forecast After 3–4 sprints the team has a stable velocity. Then "we have 60 points left, velocity is ~20, that's about 3 sprints" is a far more honest forecast than asking "how many hours?" — and it gets sharper with every sprint.

The team's daily progress radar

Sprint Burndown Chart

The Sprint Burndown shows how much work remains each day of a sprint. Y-axis = remaining work (in story points or task-hours). X-axis = sprint days. It's updated daily, usually at the standup.

Ideal burndown
Actual remaining work
✅

On track

The actual line hugs the ideal. Healthy sprint — keep doing what you're doing.

⚠️

Behind

Actual sits consistently above the ideal. Risk of not finishing — discuss in standup, consider de-scoping a story.

🚀

Ahead

Actual sits below the ideal. Either we over-estimated (calibrate next sprint) or we can pull in extra work.

The chart is a conversation, not a verdict A line going the wrong way means talk about it now, not push harder in silence. The point of the burndown is to give the team enough information mid-sprint to course-correct — not to grade them at the end.
